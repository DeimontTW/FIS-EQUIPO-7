# Guion entrega 3 #

## Product (Germán) ## 
 Can you imagine a map of the faculty where you can see the areas you are interested in, read important information, see announcements, and use it to find your way easily and quickly, especially if you are new?

 Welcome to the virtual map project of the UADY Faculty of Mathematics. This project was created with three main users in mind: future students who want to join, current students, and external visitors. It includes both a 2D view and a street-view of the faculty.

 We found a big problem: right now, there is no digital tool that allows people to see the campus or get detailed information without going there in person, especially for people who are not familiar with it.

## Requirements (Rooney) ##
Everything started with the creation of the functional and non-functional requirements. We didn’t just list the functions; we also organized them by priority, based on what our users really need to do on the map.

This first list later became more detailed user stories.

However, we had a problem: all the information was in our GitHub repository, but we didn’t have a clear way to work. We didn’t have a process.

## Process (Josué) ##

That is why we defined a process based on the Scrum methodology. Our process had three main pillars: clear role assignment, activities organized in sprints, and regular meetings to check real progress.

To manage everything, we introduced detailed logs, where we identified each member’s tasks and the tools they used.

Each sprint had to finish with a deliverable. We also recorded our progress in a performance metric to show each member’s contribution to the project clearly.

With the process under control, we moved to the design phase.

## Design (Isaac) ##

We used artificial intelligence along with the first version of our requirements to create our first prototype. This version was very important because it helped us see important details and mistakes that were not visible on paper.

Thanks to this, we improved the requirements and added strict acceptance criteria to our user stories.

But something was missing: usability. To improve it, we created “synthetic users” based on our real profiles—future student, current student, and visitor. This helped us define new non-functional requirements focused on usability.

With all this information, we built the final version using Framer and artificial intelligence. After finishing this new version, we moved on to testing.

## Testing (Leonardo Matú) ##
We carried out three different tests, one for each user type, to make sure the prototype met their expectations.

We tested the ability to search for specific locations, how easy it was to find useful information about the site, and the experience of viewing the faculty areas. Each test was designed for the needs of each user profile.

The results showed a 100% completion rate. All users were able to finish their search and navigation tasks. However, 66% had initial trouble finding the search bar, and half of the users tried to interact with the map as if it were touch-screen, which showed us a level of interactivity we had not planned for.

## Testing and conclusions (Leonardo Palma) ##

The students were the most efficient and the most satisfied, with a score of 4.3 out of 5\.

 The future students, since they did not know the faculty, showed the most variation, their learning curve was slower, taking between 5 and 11 minutes to finish the tasks.

 Lastly, the visitors. Even though 100% of them needed help at the beginning to find the tools, once they found them, they completed the tasks without problems.

Finally, what did we learn from the whole process?  
At the management level, we learned how to turn our initial chaos into a Scrum workflow that taught us to define roles and measure progress.  
In design, using prototypes and synthetic users helped us fail fast and fix early, improving our requirements.  
And from testing, we learned how to use different tools and follow the steps needed to create a software product.
